Poincaré Recurrence Theorem

Theorem (Poincaré Recurrence, strong form). If \((X, \mathscr{B}, \mu)\) is a finite measure space and T preserves \(\mu\), then for any \(B \in \mathscr{B}\) with positive measure \(\mu(B) > 0\), \(\mu\)-almost every point \(x \in B\) is infinitely recurrent to \(B\).

Proof. Let \(A\) be the set of points in \(B\) that do not return to \(B\) infinitely many times. That is, \(A =\) {\(x \in B\) such that there exists \(k \geq 1\) such that \(T^n(x) \in B\) for all \(n \geq k\)}. Consider the set \(A_0 =\) {\(x \in B\) such that \(T^n(x) \not\in B\) for all \(n>0\)} and the sets \(A_k = T^{-k}(A_0)\) for \(k \geq 1\). If \(x \in T^{-k}(A_0)\) then \(T^k(x) \in A_0\), so \(T^k(x) \in B\) and \(T^n(T^k(x)) = T^{k+n}(x) \not\in B\) for all \(n > 0\). Thus, \(A_k =\) {\(x\) such that \(T^k(x) \in B\) and \(T^n(x) \not\in B\) for all \(n>k\)}.

One can then write $$A = B \cap \bigcup_{k=0}^{+\infty}A_k$$Indeed, if \(x \in A\), let \(k\) be the largest integer such that \(T^k(x) \in B\), which is well-defined since there are only finitely many such integers by the definition of A; then \(x \in A_k\). Conversely, if \(x \in B \cap A_k\), then for some \(k\), \(T^k(x) \in B\) for all \(n > k\), so \(x\) can return only finitely many times and belongs to \(A\). By the monotonicity of a measure, $$A \subset \bigcup_{k=0}^{+\infty}A_k \implies \mu(A) \leq \mu(\bigcup_{k=0}^{+\infty}A_k)$$

Lemma. {\(A_k\)}\(_{k \in \mathbb{N}}\) are pairwise disjoint.

Proof. Suppose not. Then there would exist \(i, j\) with \(i \neq j\) such that $$A_i \cap A_j \neq \emptyset \iff \exists x \in A_i \cup A_j$$Then there would exist \(i\) such that \(T^i(x) \in B\) and \(T^n(x) \not\in B\) for \(n>i\), and \(j\) such that \(T^j(x) \in B\) and \(T^m(x) \not\in B\) for \(m>j\). Without loss of generality, suppose \(i < j\). Then \(T^i(x) \in B\) and \(T^j(x) \in B\), contradicting the definition of \(A_i\). Therefore, {\(A_k\)}\(_{k \in \mathbb{N}}\) are pairwise disjoint.

Then, by the definition of a measure and the finiteness of \(\mu\),$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\mu(A_k) = \mu\left(\bigcup_{k=0}^{+\infty}A_k\right) \leq \mu(X) < +\infty$$ Furthermore, \(\mu(A_k) = \mu(A_0)\) for all \(k \in \mathbb{N}\) since \(A_k = T^{-k}(A_0)\) and \(T\) is measure-preserving. The only possibility to have a convergent series with non-negative terms is \(\mu(A_0) = \mu(A_k) = 0\) for all \(k \in \mathbb{N}\). Then, $$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\mu(A_k) = 0 \implies \mu(A) \leq \mu\left(\bigcup_{k=0}^{+\infty}A_k\right) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\mu(A_k) = 0$$ Thus, \(\mu(A) = 0\), and almost every point in \(B\) is infinitely recurrent.
